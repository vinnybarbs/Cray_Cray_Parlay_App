# Rename exploration: parent brand for the graded-picks business

Date: July 11, 2026. Prepared for Vince. Scope per the brief. The company gets a new parent brand. "Cray Cray Parlays" is demoted to the parlay-builder feature name. "The House Ledger" stays as the name of the public settlement record no matter what. Every candidate below was tested against the sentence "Name. Every pick on The House Ledger."

Screening method. Candidates were generated across six territories, then the strongest went through a four-part knockout. Domain availability was checked live through a registrar API on July 11. Social handles, trademark exposure, and betting-space collisions were checked by web search. Web search is a knockout screen, not clearance. Before committing to any finalist, run a real USPTO search in Class 41 (entertainment services, sports information) and Class 42 (software), and hand-check the Instagram and X handles, since search engines miss small accounts.

One flag outside the assignment. Both houseledger.com and thehouseledger.com are already registered by third parties. The House Ledger name is decided and it can live at a path (brand.com/ledger), but you should know the exact-match domains are gone and check who holds them before the trademark filing.

## 3. The shortlist, ranked

### 1. TrapHawk

The story. A hawk sits above the field and sees the trap before you walk into it. One compound word that carries both halves of the product. The hawk hunts (finds the edges) and the hawk watches (calls the Traps). To a burned tailer it says something is doing surveillance on the lines for you, and it names the one feature no capper will ever copy. It is squarely in the stated taste lane, an agent-mechanic name with teeth, but tighter than two words.

The pairing. "TrapHawk. Every pick on The House Ledger." The aggressive name and the sober ledger split the two registers cleanly. The name talks like the user, the ledger talks like an auditor.

The nesting. TrapHawk > Cray Cray Parlays. The degen feature name sits naturally under a predator parent. "TrapHawk's parlay builder is called Cray Cray Parlays" explains itself in one sentence.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for TrapHawk." Passes. One word, no explanation, sounds like a real product a grown man uses, and it invites the follow-up question ("what's the trap part?") that leads straight to the pitch.

Availability. Best overall profile of the field. traphawk.com is listed on the Atom marketplace at $3,495 buy-now or $176 a month lease-to-own, domain only. traphawk.bet ($17.99) and traphawk.io ($37.99) are open, so you can launch on .bet day one and buy the .com when revenue justifies it. No social accounts, no betting service, no app, and no trademark surfaced in search. Full USPTO clearance still required.

Logo direction. Keep Sharp-Quant. The mark is a geometric hawk eye hovering over a horizontal betting line, drawn as monoline strokes with an amber diamond pupil, framed in terminal targeting brackets. It reads as surveillance hardware, not a sports mascot. Wordmark in JetBrains Mono, TRAP in amber and HAWK in white, so the moat word carries the accent color. See logo-traphawk-lockup.svg and logo-traphawk-mark.svg.

Honest risk. Said aloud it can be misheard as "Trackhawk," the Jeep, which also has traction in car-guy and rap culture. And "trap" carries a trap-music connotation that could read street-brand rather than math-brand if the visual identity ever drifts casual. The auditor-grade styling is the mitigation, and the Trap label already lives in the product vocabulary, so the association is earned.

### 2. Trap Dodger

The story. It is one of the two names Vince volunteered, and it is the most literal benefit statement in the field. The product's moat is telling you what not to bet, and this name is that sentence compressed to two words. To a burned tailer it promises the exact thing the last capper never gave him, protection from his own worst clicks.

The pairing. "Trap Dodger. Every pick on The House Ledger." Works. The playful agent name against the deadpan ledger is the two-register voice again.

The nesting. Trap Dodger > Cray Cray Parlays. Fine, though two playful names stacked is slightly goofier than the TrapHawk version.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for Trap Dodger." Passes with a smile. It is charming and self-aware, which suits the audience, but it sounds a notch more like a browser game than TrapHawk does.

Availability. Cheapest path in the field. trapdodger.com is open at $11.25, with .bet, .io, and .app all open behind it. No accounts, no company, no betting collision. The only exact-name uses are minor video game items.

Logo direction. Sharp-Quant holds. The mark is a dashed amber route that steps around an X hazard marker and continues on, a path that refuses the trap. It doubles as a chart annotation, which keeps it in the terminal world. See logo-trapdodger-lockup.svg and logo-trapdodger-mark.svg.

Honest risk. Two real ones. First, the name only sells defense. It says you will lose less, not that the model finds winners at 63.5 percent, so the offense half of the product has to be carried by copy. Second, Los Angeles Dodgers LLC holds 52 registrations, polices aggressively, and this is a sports service. A composite mark with "Dodger" in it probably survives, but an opposition letter is a live possibility and every search for the brand fights through Dodgers baseball content forever.

### 3. Fade the House

The story. The whole promise in three words. The house always wins, so this brand exists to bet against the house with math. It is a rally cry, and rally cries recruit. For a burned tailer it reframes the enemy, not the capper but the book itself, and positions the product as the tool for the fight.

The pairing. "Fade the House. Every pick on The House Ledger." The double House is either a deliberate brand system built on one word or a tangle, and honestly it is a little of both. It scans well aloud, and "we fade the house, and we keep the ledger to prove it" is a coherent story, but see the risk below.

The nesting. Fade the House > Cray Cray Parlays. Acceptable, though a phrase parent over a phrase feature is wordy in navigation.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for Fade the House." Passes and is the most quotable of the field. It sounds like joining a side, which is strong for community-led growth.

Availability. fadethehouse.com is open at $11.25 and fadethehouse.bet at $17.99. No podcast, service, or trademark surfaced. Clean.

Logo direction. Two concepts. On-palette, a monoline house glyph struck through by an amber slash, wordmark in mono beneath. Off-palette option worth considering, a receipt treatment on paper white with ink type and an amber GRADED stamp, because this brand's proof asset is literally a receipt and one light-mode asset would own that visual in a feed of dark degen graphics. See logo-fadethehouse-lockup.svg and logo-fadethehouse-mark.svg.

Honest risk. The semantic tangle is real. If the brand's job is to fade the house, a product called The House Ledger makes people ask whose house we are talking about. It is explainable, but a brand that needs a paragraph of explanation at first contact is spending trust it has not earned. Phrase names are also weaker trademarks and worse app-store citizens than compound words.

### 4. The Fade Desk

The story. A trading desk for fades and plays. "Desk" imports institutional seriousness, "fade" imports degen fluency, so the name is the two-register voice in two words. It suggests an operation, not a guy with a Telegram channel.

The pairing. "The Fade Desk. Every pick on The House Ledger." Clean, and the desk-and-ledger imagery is internally consistent, like a small trading firm publishing its book.

The nesting. The Fade Desk > Cray Cray Parlays. Good contrast, serious parent over degen feature.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for The Fade Desk." Passes. Sounds like a service with employees.

Availability. fadedesk.com is registered but shows no active service, so the operating risk is low. thefadedesk.com is open at $11.25 and fadedesk.bet at $17.99. No collisions or marks surfaced.

Logo direction. Sharp-Quant native. Stacked ledger rows as the mark with one row struck in amber, the faded line, wordmark in mono. Almost designs itself inside the existing terminal aesthetic.

Honest risk. Outside betting, "fade" means a haircut, and "The Fade Desk" could read barbershop to a cold audience. Inside the audience it is unambiguous. The name also centers fading when the product's headline stat is picking winners.

### 5. Sharp Receipts

The story. The receipts brand said plainly. "Sharp" is the identity the customer aspires to, "receipts" is the proof he demands. It is the only finalist that names the settlement mechanic itself.

The pairing. "Sharp Receipts. Every pick on The House Ledger." Slightly redundant, since receipts and ledger are the same idea twice, but redundancy on the trust message is a defensible choice for this audience.

The nesting. Sharp Receipts > Cray Cray Parlays. Fine.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for Sharp Receipts." Passes but flat. It describes the product more than it brands it.

Availability. sharpreceipts.com open at $11.25. Nothing surfaced anywhere. Clean because nobody has wanted it, which cuts both ways.

Logo direction. The receipt is the mark, a perforated-edge ticket in monoline with an amber settled tick. Works on-palette or as the light paper treatment described under Fade the House.

Honest risk. "Sharp" is the single most overworked word in betting tools, and out of context the name sounds like expense software. It is a strong tagline wearing a company name's clothes.

### 6. Closing Number

The story. The closing number is the most respected object in sharp culture. Beating it is the definition of a real edge. The name signals that the founder knows the culture, and it flatters the customer as someone who does too.

The pairing. "Closing Number. Every pick on The House Ledger." Sober and credible, the most grown-up pairing in the field.

The nesting. Closing Number > Cray Cray Parlays. The starkest register contrast of any finalist, which could be a feature.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for Closing Number." Passes but with no spark. It sounds like a newsletter.

Availability. closingnumber.com open at $11.25, closingnumber.bet open. No name collisions, but Unabated, Pikkit, OddsJam, and NumberEdge all actively market CLV territory, so the concept neighborhood is crowded even though the name is free.

Logo direction. Numerals as the mark, a large amber figure with tabular mono styling and a settlement underline. Cleanest possible fit with the existing aesthetic.

Honest risk. It aims at the wrong persona. The audit was explicit that the sharp-curious are the secondary audience and courting them drags the copy toward jargon. The burned tailer does not know what a closing number is, and the product does not actually sell CLV tracking. Promise mismatch.

### 7. Degen Audit (wildcard)

The story. The two-register voice as a literal compound noun. Degen on the outside, audit on the inside. It tells the burned tailer exactly what this is, the picks habit he already has, submitted to accounting.

The pairing. "Degen Audit. Every pick on The House Ledger." Coherent, an audit publishes to a ledger.

The nesting. Degen Audit > Cray Cray Parlays. Works, same voice family.

Podcast test. "I pay twenty bucks a month for Degen Audit." Memorable and funny, and the host will riff on it, which is free marketing.

Availability. degenaudit.com open at $11.25. No company or mark. Crypto Twitter uses "degen audit" loosely for meme-coin contract reviews, so there is vocabulary pollution but no operator.

Logo direction. Break the palette deliberately or do not do this name. A rubber-stamp treatment, DEGEN in spray-can-adjacent block letters struck through by a clean amber AUDIT stamp. It is a costume, and it commits the whole brand to the joke.

Honest risk. "Degen" is a moment-in-time word that is already aging, it smells of crypto, and the name is ambiguous about who gets audited. It also caps the ceiling. A multi-product company called Degen Audit cannot ever sell to a partner or a bank with a straight face. Kept on the list because it is the honest wildcard, not because it should win.

## 4. Recommendation

Rename to TrapHawk. Second choice, Trap Dodger.

The reasoning. The moat is the Trap label, so the name should own the word Trap, and only three viable candidates do. Trapline is dead on domains and genericness. Between TrapHawk and Trap Dodger, TrapHawk wins on three counts. It carries offense and defense in one image while Trap Dodger only carries defense. It has no trademark bully in its lane while Trap Dodger shares a word with the most enforcement-happy club in baseball. And it compresses to one word, which is better in an app icon, a handle, and a sentence. The $3,495 for traphawk.com is real money but it is one month of the marketing budget for a permanent asset, and launching on traphawk.bet first is a legitimate bridge since .bet actively helps this brand. TrapHawk is also squarely inside the taste Vince expressed, the evocative agent-mechanic name, just sharpened from two words to one.

Trap Dodger is the second choice rather than the winner for exactly the two reasons flagged above, defense-only messaging and the Dodgers exposure. If clearance counsel later kills TrapHawk, Trap Dodger with the .com in hand at $11.25 is a fast, in-taste fallback, and the counsel review should price the Dodgers question at the same time.

Fade the House is the best pure marketing line in the field and should live on as campaign language regardless of the company name. "Fade the house. Audit our 63.5, then ask them for theirs." It works harder as a slogan under TrapHawk than as a name over The House Ledger.

Immediate next steps if TrapHawk holds up overnight: register traphawk.bet and traphawk.io today (under $60 total), claim @traphawk on Instagram, X, and TikTok by hand, order a USPTO knockout search in Classes 41 and 42 from the trademark counsel already engaged for the House Ledger filing, and only then open the Atom negotiation for the .com, since owning the socials first improves your bargaining position.
