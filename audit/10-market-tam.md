# Market size and pricing (audit phase 1)

Researched 2026-07-11 via multi-source web research with adversarial claim
verification (103 agents, 3-vote verification per claim; refuted claims
excluded and listed at the bottom). All prices captured 2026-07-11; this
market reprices often, recheck before shipping a pricing change.

## Decision

Launch at $19.99/month with the 7-day trial, as planned. It sits exactly on
the verified entry-tier anchor (BetQL Starter $19.99, Outlier Premium
$19.99), the trial length matches segment convention, and the verified
track record converts better as a trust asset at the anchor price than as
justification for a premium price with zero retention history. Follow-ons:
an annual plan at $119.99 to $149.99 (consumer-tier norm is 40 to 75
percent off monthly), and a second tier at $39.99 to $49.99 later, gated on
a full season of published ledger, instead of ever raising the base.

## Market size (assumptions labeled)

- Past-year US sports bettors: 53 to 70 million adults depending on survey
  (AGA 21 percent of adults, NerdWallet/Harris 20 percent, Pew 22 percent,
  Siena 27 percent with active online accounts). Four independent surveys
  converge on 20 to 22 percent participation, still growing fast (Siena
  active-account trend: 19 percent in 2024, 22 percent in 2025, 27 percent
  in 2026). High confidence.
- The tighter SAM denominator: Pew found 10 percent of US adults bet online
  specifically, roughly 26 million people. That is the population that can
  buy an online picks subscription. High confidence.
- Spend: bettors self-report $3,284 mean and $750 median annual gambling
  spend (Harris/NerdWallet, n=458, all gambling not just sports). A $240
  annual subscription is about 7 percent of the mean and 32 percent of the
  median. Priced for the engaged middle, not whales. High confidence.
- Top-down anchor: US sports betting operator revenue estimated at $19.76B
  for 2025 (Grand View Research; runs above AGA-reported ~$13.7B GGR for
  2024, treat as upper bound). This is the economy the product sits beside,
  not the subscription TAM.
- SOM: [ASSUMPTION] No verified source quantifies what share of bettors pay
  for picks or tools. At an illustrative 1 to 3 percent attach rate on the
  26M online-bettor SAM at $240/year, the paid picks/tools segment is
  roughly $60M to $190M per year. A realistic first-year SOM for a
  single-tier newcomer is thousands of subscribers, not tens of thousands.
  Low confidence; the attach rate is the single biggest hole.

Plan against the SOM, not the TAM. 1,000 subscribers at $19.99 is ~$240K
ARR; 5,000 is ~$1.2M ARR.

## Why the deltas are so large

The market is three different products under one label. Entry consumer
tiers ($19.99 to $49.99) sell guidance and confidence to casual bettors,
priced like streaming apps against an entertainment budget. Professional
terminals ($99 to $799) sell real-time data and edge-detection, priced
against expected betting profit. Community and white-glove offerings price
on access and insider status. The $19.99 buyer and the $799 buyer are
different customers with different jobs. Cray Cray is a consumer product
with a professional-grade trust asset, which is a positioning gap: nobody
verified at the $19.99 tier publishes a math-graded settlement ledger.
